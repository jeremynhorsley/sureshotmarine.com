Professional Background

My career in electrical and electronic systems began in 1995 as an avionics technician in the U.S. Navy aboard the USS Enterprise. From 1995–1999, I worked in operational environments where system failures were not inconveniences — they were mission risks.

After active duty, I worked in field service roles through 2007, diagnosing and supporting complex electrical systems in real-world conditions. That period refined a structured approach to troubleshooting: define the symptom, capture measurements, isolate the cause, document the result.

I later rejoined federal service with the U.S. Coast Guard, including time at the National Motor Lifeboat School in Astoria, Oregon, and service aboard the USCGC Aspen in San Francisco as an electronics technician. These assignments reinforced reliability under demanding marine conditions — vibration, corrosion, weather exposure, and operational pressure.

While serving, I completed a Bachelor of Science in Electrical Engineering, formalizing the technical foundation behind years of hands-on field experience.

  • AC and DC power distribution
  • Battery systems and charging infrastructure
  • Ground fault detection and isolation
  • Power quality troubleshooting
  • Monitoring, alarms, and controls
  • Structured preventative maintenance programs

Today, I apply that same reliability-first methodology to marine electrical systems throughout the San Francisco Bay Area.

Boating & On-Water Experience

Throughout my career, I have been an active boat owner and sailor. Time on the water has never been separate from the work — it has informed it.

I have maintained and upgraded my own vessels for decades, sailing in both Pacific Northwest and San Francisco Bay conditions. That firsthand experience provides practical understanding of aging wiring, layered electronics, limited access, corrosion, and real-world operating demands.

Marine electrical systems are not laboratory systems. They live in motion, salt air, temperature swings, and tight compartments. Solutions must be durable, understandable, and serviceable — not just theoretically correct.

The objective is simple: restore clarity, stability, and confidence so your system performs when underway and remains predictable at the dock.

The Name “Sure Shot”

Sure Shot is the name of my 1990 Ericson 32-200 — a capable coastal cruiser from the final production years of Ericson Yachts. Like many boats of that era, her systems reflect decades of upgrades, retrofits, and evolving standards. I continue to maintain, evaluate, and upgrade her electrical and onboard systems myself. That ongoing work reinforces the realities of marine environments: corrosion, undocumented modifications, mixed AC/DC systems, and well-intentioned wiring changes layered over time. Living with and improving those systems firsthand shapes how I approach every vessel — understand the history, verify the wiring, measure performance under load, and restore clarity before making changes. The business carries her name as a reflection of that discipline.
